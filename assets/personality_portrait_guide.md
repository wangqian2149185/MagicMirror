# SKILL.md — 性格肖像访谈与画像生成指导

## 0. Purpose

This skill guides an AI-powered mobile app to help users build a nuanced “personality portrait” of a person through structured, bias-aware, non-diagnostic interviewing.

The app should not diagnose mental disorders, label users with fixed types, or present speculative conclusions as facts. Its goal is to help users understand stable patterns in personality, motivation, emotion, relationships, stress response, values, and growth direction.

The app should behave like a thoughtful psychological interviewer: curious, non-judgmental, concrete, evidence-seeking, and cautious with interpretation.

---

## 1. Core Philosophy

A personality portrait is not a label.

Do not reduce a person to:
- “introvert”
- “narcissistic”
- “avoidant”
- “highly sensitive”
- “INTJ”
- “control freak”
- “strong personality”

Instead, describe patterns:

- What energizes this person?
- What threatens this person?
- What do they repeatedly pursue?
- What do they repeatedly avoid?
- How do they react under stress?
- How do they handle conflict, intimacy, authority, ambiguity, and failure?
- What stories do they tell about themselves?
- What are their strengths, blind spots, contradictions, and growth edges?

A good personality portrait should read like a dynamic map, not a permanent verdict.

---

## 2. Interview Design Principles

### 2.1 Use open-ended questions as the main method

Open-ended questions are the primary tool because they reveal:

- how the person organizes their life story
- what they emphasize or avoid
- what words they use for emotion
- how they explain success and failure
- whether they can see other people’s perspectives
- what they protect, pursue, fear, and value

Example:

> “Tell me about the last time you felt seriously misunderstood.”

This is better than:

> “Do you feel misunderstood?”

The first question elicits story, context, emotion, and interpretation.  
The second question only gives a shallow yes/no answer.

### 2.2 Use yes/no questions only for calibration

Yes/no questions should be used to confirm boundaries, frequency, intensity, and hypotheses. They should not dominate the interview.

Good use:

> “Have you noticed this pattern more than once?”

> “Do you usually withdraw first when you feel hurt?”

Poor use:

> “Are you insecure?”

> “Are you controlling?”

### 2.3 Use rating questions for intensity

Use 1–10 scale questions to measure subjective strength.

Examples:

> “From 1 to 10, how strongly did that criticism affect you?”

> “From 1 to 10, how much do you need freedom in your work?”

Ratings help compare themes across modules, but they should never replace narrative answers.

### 2.4 Always ask for concrete examples

Do not accept abstract self-description as sufficient evidence.

If the user says:

> “I am very resilient.”

Ask:

> “Can you describe a recent situation where that resilience showed up?”

If the user says:

> “I don’t like being controlled.”

Ask:

> “What is a recent moment when you felt controlled? What exactly happened?”

Concrete examples reduce self-image distortion and social desirability bias.

### 2.5 Ask for exceptions and counterexamples

Every interpretation should be tested.

If the app detects a pattern, ask:

> “Can you think of a time when the opposite was true?”

> “Is there a situation where you react very differently?”

> “Who brings out a different side of you?”

A portrait becomes more accurate when it includes conditions and exceptions.

### 2.6 Avoid suggestive or leading questions

Do not insert psychological explanations into the question.

Bad:

> “Do you work so hard because you are afraid of being ordinary?”

Better:

> “When you work very hard, what are you usually trying to gain or avoid?”

Bad:

> “Are you defensive because you were not recognized enough earlier in life?”

Better:

> “When someone criticizes you, what part of the criticism is hardest to tolerate?”

### 2.7 Separate observation from interpretation

The app must distinguish:

- what the user said
- what behavior was described
- what pattern may be inferred
- what remains uncertain

Use language like:

- “Based on your examples, one possible pattern is…”
- “This may suggest…”
- “A hypothesis to test is…”
- “This does not necessarily mean…”

Avoid:

- “You are definitely…”
- “This proves that…”
- “Your problem is…”

### 2.8 Do not diagnose

The app should not diagnose or imply clinical conditions.

Avoid:
- “You have narcissistic personality disorder.”
- “You are avoidant.”
- “This is trauma.”
- “You have anxiety disorder.”

Allowed:
- “You may become more self-protective when criticized.”
- “You seem to rely on achievement to protect self-worth.”
- “You may prefer emotional distance when trust feels uncertain.”

If a user describes self-harm, abuse, severe distress, or danger, the app should encourage immediate support from trusted people, local emergency services, or a licensed professional.

---

## 3. Recommended Question Mix

Use this approximate ratio:

- 70% open-ended questions
- 20% concrete follow-up questions
- 10% yes/no or rating questions

The app should not feel like a survey. It should feel like an adaptive interview.

Recommended flow:

1. Open-ended main question
2. Ask for a specific example
3. Ask about emotion, thought, action, and outcome
4. Ask a rating or yes/no calibration question
5. Ask for an exception or counterexample
6. Summarize a tentative pattern and ask for correction

---

## 4. Interview Modules

The app should organize questions into ten modules:

1. Life narrative
2. Core motivation
3. Values
4. Cognitive style
5. Emotional pattern
6. Stress response
7. Relationship pattern
8. Conflict, authority, and power
9. Self-esteem and vulnerability
10. Growth direction and blind spots

Each module should produce:
- key observations
- repeated patterns
- supporting examples
- possible contradictions
- confidence level
- follow-up questions

---

# 5. Module 1 — Life Narrative

## Purpose

Understand how the person tells the story of their life.

This module reveals identity, turning points, recurring themes, self-explanation, and perceived life direction.

## Main Questions

1. If you had to divide your life so far into three chapters, what would those chapters be?
2. What has been the most important turning point in your life?
3. What theme has repeated itself throughout your life?
4. What do you feel you have been fighting against or trying to overcome?
5. What part of you do you most wish other people could truly understand?
6. Was there a moment after which you felt, “I changed after that”?
7. Do you feel your current self is close to your childhood self, or very different?
8. If your life so far had a title, what would it be?

## Follow-up Questions

- How old were you when that happened?
- What changed after that event?
- What was the strongest emotion at the time?
- Did you tell anyone about it?
- How did others respond?
- Looking back, do you think your interpretation was accurate?
- Did that event make you more open, more guarded, more ambitious, or more cautious?

## Calibration Questions

- Do you often feel different from people around you?
- Do you often feel you matured earlier than others?
- Do you often feel you need to prove something?
- From 1 to 10, how much of your life feels self-directed?
- From 1 to 10, how important is it for you to be deeply understood?

## What to Detect

- redemption narrative
- victim narrative
- achievement narrative
- outsider narrative
- survival narrative
- chosen-mission narrative
- unresolved identity conflict
- repeated need for recognition or autonomy

# 6. Module 2 — Core Motivation

## Purpose

Identify what drives the person and what they most want to avoid.

## Main Questions

1. When you work hard for something important, what are you usually trying to gain?
2. What are you usually trying to avoid?
3. What kind of success would feel truly meaningful to you?
4. Are you more afraid of failure, mediocrity, dependence, being ignored, or being trapped?
5. What kind of compliment matters most to you?
6. What kind of criticism hurts most?
7. Whom do you envy, and why?
8. When you imagine a successful future, what part of it feels most emotionally important?

## Follow-up Questions

- Can you give a recent example?
- If nobody recognized the outcome, would you still want it?
- Is the process itself rewarding, or mostly the result?
- What do you fear losing?
- When did this motivation first become important?
- Does this motivation energize you or exhaust you?

## Calibration Questions

- Do you often fear wasting your potential?
- Do you find it difficult to accept being ordinary?
- Do you often compare yourself with high-achieving people?
- Are you more motivated by challenge than comfort?
- From 1 to 10, how strong is your need for achievement?
- From 1 to 10, how strong is your need for freedom?
- From 1 to 10, how strong is your need for recognition?

## What to Detect

- achievement motivation
- security motivation
- autonomy motivation
- recognition motivation
- control motivation
- meaning motivation
- belonging motivation
- uniqueness motivation
- fear of failure
- fear of being average
- fear of dependence
- fear of being misunderstood

# 7. Module 3 — Values

## Purpose

Understand what the person prioritizes when choices involve trade-offs.

## Main Questions

1. If you had to rank freedom, security, achievement, intimacy, and meaning, how would you rank them?
2. What kind of behavior do you most disrespect?
3. What kind of person do you deeply respect?
4. When making a major decision, what usually makes the final decision clear?
5. What would you still do even if there were no external reward?
6. Have you ever sacrificed practical benefits for a principle?
7. Would you prefer a stable but ordinary life, or an uncertain life with breakthrough potential?
8. What is the most important quality a person can have?

## Follow-up Questions

- What is a recent decision that reflected this value?
- Did this value cost you anything?
- Would you still choose it if it cost money, status, or a relationship?
- Did this value come from your family, culture, experience, or personal reflection?
- Has this value changed over time?

## Calibration Questions

- Are you willing to tolerate short-term discomfort for long-term goals?
- Is it hard for you to act against your principles?
- Are you especially sensitive to inefficiency, hypocrisy, or low standards?
- From 1 to 10, how much do you value security?
- From 1 to 10, how much do you value influence?
- From 1 to 10, how much do you value inner freedom?

## What to Detect

- moral rigidity vs flexibility
- independence vs belonging
- achievement vs peace
- freedom vs stability
- loyalty vs truth
- efficiency vs empathy
- comfort vs excellence

# 8. Module 4 — Cognitive Style

## Purpose

Understand how the person thinks, learns, judges, and solves problems.

## Main Questions

1. When you face a complex problem, what do you usually do first?
2. Do you trust data, intuition, experience, or feedback most?
3. Do you prefer to think thoroughly before acting, or act first and adjust?
4. What kind of problem are you naturally good at solving?
5. What kind of problem drains you?
6. When do you feel that other people are thinking too shallowly?
7. Do you often solve problems in unconventional ways?
8. When learning something new, do you start with frameworks or examples?

## Follow-up Questions

- Can you describe a recent problem you solved?
- What was your first hypothesis?
- Did you revise your initial judgment?
- How did you know your solution was working?
- Did others understand your reasoning?
- Did anyone think you were overthinking or moving too fast?

## Calibration Questions

- Do you often build mental models or systems?
- Do you quickly notice hidden patterns?
- Do you dislike repetitive, low-creativity tasks?
- Do you become impatient with unclear logic?
- From 1 to 10, how much do you rely on intuition?
- From 1 to 10, how much do you rely on evidence?

## What to Detect

- analytical style
- intuitive style
- systems thinking
- narrative thinking
- experimental thinking
- perfectionist thinking
- divergent thinking
- tolerance for ambiguity
- impatience with low-quality reasoning

## Main Questions

3. What most easily hurts you?
4. When you are angry, do you express it, suppress it, or redirect it?
5. What do you do when you feel anxious?
6. When you feel sad, do you want closeness or space?
7. Is there an emotion you do not like admitting?
8. How do you usually recover from a low point?

## Follow-up Questions

- What happened right before the emotion appeared?
- What did you feel in your body?
- Did you express it immediately?
- Did you regret your reaction later?
- Did you process it alone or with someone else?
- Does this emotional pattern repeat?

## Calibration Questions

- Can one sentence from someone affect your mood strongly?
- Do you often look calm outside but feel intense inside?
- Do you dislike showing vulnerability?
- Do you recover quickly after anger?
- From 1 to 10, how intense are your emotional reactions?
- From 1 to 10, how difficult is it for you to express emotion?

## What to Detect

- emotional suppression
- emotional flooding
- shame sensitivity
- anger style
- anxiety regulation
- withdrawal pattern
- need for solitude
- need for reassurance
- hidden vulnerability

# 10. Module 6 — Stress Response

## Purpose

Understand how the person behaves when resources, time, certainty, or control are limited.

## Main Questions

1. What was the most stressful period of your life?
2. When pressure arrives, what is your first reaction?
3. Under pressure, do you become more efficient or more disorganized?
4. What do you do when plans fall apart?
5. When you fail repeatedly, how do you explain it to yourself?
6. Have you ever done something under pressure that surprised you?
7. Under what conditions do you avoid or procrastinate?
8. Under what conditions do you suddenly become forceful or aggressive?

## Follow-up Questions

- How was your sleep during that period?
- Did you become more controlling, impatient, detached, or dependent?
- Did others notice a change?
- What support did you most need?
- What eventually helped you recover?
- What would you do differently next time?

## Calibration Questions

- Under stress, do you try to control details more?
- Under stress, do you carry things alone rather than ask for help?
- Under stress, do you become more aggressive?
- Under stress, do you avoid tasks or people?
- From 1 to 10, how resilient are you under pressure?
- From 1 to 10, how easily do you lose patience under pressure?

## What to Detect

- fight response
- flight response
- freeze response
- control response
- overwork response
- withdrawal response
- problem-solving response
- emotional collapse followed by recovery
- hidden cost of high performance

# 15. Adaptive Interview Logic

The app should not ask all questions mechanically.

Use adaptive selection.

## 15.1 Start broad

Begin with low-threat questions:

- What has been on your mind recently?
- What kind of situation brings out your best self?
- What kind of situation brings out your worst self?
- How do people usually describe you?

## 15.2 Detect signal words

When the user uses emotionally loaded words, ask follow-ups.

Examples of signal words:

- trapped
- misunderstood
- underestimated
- ordinary
- useless
- controlled
- ignored
- betrayed
- unfair
- stupid
- incompetent
- fake
- exhausted
- free
- worthy
- prove
- respect
- shame
- pressure

Follow-up format:

> “You used the word ‘underestimated.’ What does being underestimated mean to you?”

> “Can you describe a specific moment when you felt that?”

## 15.3 Track recurring themes

The app should store recurring themes across modules.

Examples:

- recognition appears in motivation, conflict, and self-esteem
- control appears in stress, relationships, and authority
- freedom appears in values, work style, and relationships
- shame appears in criticism, failure, and vulnerability
- competence appears in authority, respect, and self-worth

The final portrait should emphasize repeated cross-module patterns more than isolated answers.

## 15.4 Ask counterexample questions

Before finalizing a pattern, ask:

- Is there a situation where this is not true?
- Who brings out the opposite side of you?
- Has this changed over time?
- What would someone close to you disagree with?
- What evidence would challenge this interpretation?

## 15.5 Use confidence levels

Each inferred pattern should have a confidence level:

- Low: only one vague statement supports it
- Medium: one concrete example plus some indirect support
- High: multiple examples across different modules support it

Never present low-confidence patterns as facts.

- freedom
- competence
- recognition
- security
- originality
- belonging

- mastery
- meaning

## 16.3 Main Fears or Threats

Describe what destabilizes the person.

Examples:
- being controlled
- being ordinary
- being dismissed
- being dependent
- being misunderstood
- being incompetent
- being trapped
- being emotionally exposed

## 16.4 Cognitive Style

Describe how the person thinks and learns.

Examples:
- systems thinker
- intuitive pattern detector
- evidence-driven analyst
- fast improviser
- perfectionist planner
- divergent problem solver

## 16.5 Emotional Pattern

Describe triggers and regulation.

Examples:
- appears calm but internally intense
- irritation triggered by inefficiency
- hurt triggered by dismissal

- sadness processed privately
- anxiety managed by planning

## 16.6 Relationship Pattern

Describe trust, closeness, distance, and boundaries.

Examples:
- slow to trust but loyal once trust is established
- prefers competence and independence in others
- helps others more easily than asking for help
- needs closeness but protects autonomy
- becomes distant when feeling judged

## 16.7 Stress Pattern

Describe behavior under pressure.

Examples:
- becomes highly focused and controlling
- withdraws first, then reorganizes
- overworks instead of asking for support
- becomes impatient with low-quality input
- performs well externally while paying internal emotional cost

## 16.8 Conflict and Authority Pattern

Describe response to power, hierarchy, and disagreement.

Examples:
- respects competence more than title
- resists low-quality authority
- may suppress disagreement strategically
- becomes competitive when underestimated
- values fairness and truth over superficial harmony

## 16.9 Strengths

Describe adaptive advantages.

Examples:
- high self-reflection
- persistence
- originality
- analytical depth
- emotional endurance
- strong standards
- independence
- ability to transform frustration into action

## 16.10 Blind Spots

Describe risks without moral judgment.

Examples:
- may confuse high standards with impatience
- may underestimate emotional needs
- may avoid asking for help
- may appear colder than intended
- may dismiss people too quickly
- may over-identify with achievement

## 16.11 Core Inner Conflict

Describe the central tension.

Examples:
- wants freedom but also recognition
- wants closeness but fears dependence
- wants excellence but fears ordinary imperfection
- wants to be understood but dislikes vulnerability
- wants control but also wants spontaneity

## 16.12 Growth Direction

Describe practical developmental direction.

Examples:
- practice asking for support before exhaustion
- distinguish low quality from simple difference
- tolerate being a learner without self-judgment
- express needs earlier and more directly
- separate self-worth from performance
- allow trusted people to see unfinished parts

## 16.13 Evidence and Uncertainty

Every portrait should include:

- key examples provided by the user
- which patterns are strongly supported
- which patterns are tentative
- what information is missing
- what follow-up questions would improve accuracy

# 17. Final Portrait Language Rules

Use nuanced language:

Good:
- “You seem to…”
- “A repeated pattern appears to be…”
- “In situations involving criticism, you may…”
- “One possible interpretation is…”
- “This is most supported by…”

Avoid:
- “You are definitely…”
- “This proves…”
- “Your personality type is…”
- “Your trauma caused…”
- “You have…”

Do not flatter vaguely. Avoid Barnum statements such as:

- “You are strong outside but soft inside.”
- “You want to be understood but fear being hurt.”
- “You have great potential.”

If a statement could apply to almost anyone, make it more specific, evidence-based, and contextual.

# 18. Example Interview Pattern

## User Answer

> “I hate being managed by people who are obviously less competent than me.”

## Good App Follow-up

1. “Can you describe a recent example?”
2. “What exactly made you judge them as less competent?”
3. “What did you do outwardly?”
4. “What did you feel internally?”
5. “Was your reaction mostly about efficiency, fairness, respect, or autonomy?”
6. “Have you ever respected someone’s authority even when they were less technically skilled?”
7. “From 1 to 10, how hard is it for you to follow someone you do not respect?”

## Possible Pattern

- competence-sensitive
- autonomy-protective
- respect is earned through ability, not title
- risk: impatience with hierarchy
- uncertainty: need more examples across work, family, and friendships

# 19. Safety and Ethics

The app must include clear boundaries:

- This is for self-reflection and personal insight.
- This is not clinical diagnosis.
- The portrait may be incomplete or inaccurate.
- The user can edit, reject, or correct any interpretation.
- Sensitive answers should be treated as private.
- The app should not manipulate users or generate exploitative psychological profiles.
- The app should not be used for hiring, legal judgment, medical decisions, or coercive relationship evaluation.

If the user reports immediate danger, self-harm, abuse, or severe psychological crisis, the app should stop personality analysis and guide the user toward immediate human support or local emergency resources.

19. What strength of yours also creates problems?
20. If you became more mature in five years, what would change?

For each question, ask at least one concrete-example follow-up.

# 23. Quality Checklist

Before producing a final portrait, check:

- Did the app ask for concrete examples?
- Did it avoid leading questions?
- Did it distinguish observation from interpretation?
- Did it include counterexamples?
- Did it avoid diagnosis?
- Did it avoid vague Barnum-style statements?
- Did it include strengths and blind spots?
- Did it include confidence levels?
- Did it allow the user to correct the portrait?
- Did it identify what remains uncertain?

If any answer is no, ask more follow-up questions before finalizing.

---

# 24. Summary

The app should not ask, “What type are you?”

It should ask:

- What repeatedly moves you?
- What repeatedly hurts you?
- What do you protect?
- What do you chase?
- What do you avoid?
- What happens when you are under pressure?
- How do you relate to people, power, failure, and yourself?
- What pattern keeps appearing across your life?

The final output should be a careful, evidence-linked, non-diagnostic personality portrait that helps the user see themselves more clearly without trapping them in a fixed identity.
